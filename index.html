Kalpana Chawla was an Indian-born American astronaut and aerospace engineer who was the first woman of Indian origin to fly to space. She first flew on Space Shuttle Columbia in 1997 as a mission specialist and primary robotic arm operator.

Kalpana Chawla was an Indian-American astronaut and the first woman of Indian origin in space. Born on March 17, 1962, in Karnal, India, she pursued a degree in aeronautical engineering before moving to the United States for further studies.

Kalpana Chawla earned a master's degree in aerospace engineering and a Ph.D. in aerospace engineering from the University of Colorado. She later became a naturalized U.S. citizen and joined NASA in 1994.

Her first space mission was aboard the Space Shuttle Columbia in 1997, where she conducted various experiments and became a role model for aspiring astronauts. Tragically, during her second mission in 2003, the Space Shuttle Columbia disintegrated during re-entry into Earth's atmosphere. Kalpana Chawla, along with her six fellow crew members, lost her life in this tragic accident on February 1, 2003.

Kalpana Chawla's life and achievements continue to inspire people, especially in India, and she is remembered as a symbol of courage and dedication in the field of space exploration.

    Kalpana Chawla flew on two space missions. Her first mission was in 1997, as a mission specialist on the Space Shuttle Columbia during STS-87. Her second and final mission, STS-107, tragically ended in the Space Shuttle Columbia disaster in 2003.

  4. Research Contributions:

    During her time in space, Chawla conducted various experiments and contributed to scientific research. Her work on microgravity effects on different materials and life sciences experiments added valuable data to the understanding of space conditions.
